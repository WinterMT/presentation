Savage (1954)

There is no doubt that Savage’s expected utility representation theorem is very powerful. There are, however, two important questions to ask about whether Savage achieves his aims: 1) Does Savage characterise rational preferences, at least in the generic sense? And 2) Does Savage’s theorem tell us how to make rational decisions in the real world? Savage’s theory has problems meeting these two demands, taken together. Arguably the core weakness of the theory is that its various constraints and assumptions pull in different directions when it comes to constructing realistic decision models, and furthermore, at least one constraint (notably, the Sure Thing Principle) is only plausible under decision modelling assumptions that are supposed to be the output, not the input, of the theory.

Random utility theory

Thurstone (1927)

– Formulated a 'law' of comparative judgement to describe how an agent makes a judgment to differentiate between two stimuli (options)

– Based the 'just noticeable differece' from Weber-Fechner law; to select one of the alternatives the agent must notice a difference

– Hypothesized a continuous normally distributed psychological scale of discriminal processes for judging any stimulus

– Defines the comparison between two stimuli for a single agent as a function of the psycholgical scale values, the dispersion of judgements about each, and the correlation between comparisons of the two

– Extends his definition from the agent-level to the group-level

EVALUATION

FAWCETT, J. (1993). Analysis and evaluation of nursing theories. Philadelphia: F.A. Davis (36)

 

Questions for evaluation

1. Is the theory significant? [SIGNIFICANCE]

a. Are the Metaparadigm concepts and propositions addressed by the theory explicit?

b. Are the philosophical claims on which the theory is based explicit?

c. Is the conceptual model from which the theory was derived explicit?

d. Are the authors of knowledge from adjunctive disciplines acknowledged and are bibliographical citations given?

2. Is the theory internally consistent? [CONSISTENCY]

a. Are all elements of the work (philosophical claims, conceptual model, theory) congruent?

b. Do the concepts reflect semantic clarity and consistency?

c. Are there any redundant concepts?

d. Do the propositions reflect structural consistency?

3. Is the theory parsimonious? [CLARITY]

a. Is the theory stated clearly and concisely?

4. Is the theory testable? [ADEQUACY]

a. Can the concepts be observed empirically?

b. Can the proposition be measured?

5. Is the theory empirically adequate? [ADEQUACY]

a. Are the theoretical assertions congruent with empirical evidence?

6. Is the theory pragmatically adequate? [FEASIBILITY]

a. Are education and special/skill training required prior to application of the theory in clinical practice?

b. For what clinical problems is the theory appropriate?

c. Is it feasible to implement clinical protocols derived from the theory?

d. Are the nursing actions compatible with expectations for nursing practice?

e. Does the clinician have the legal ability to implement the nursing actions?

f. Do the nursing actions lead to favorable outcomes?

Experimental design

Connectedness

A design is considered connected if, given any two treatment effects Τi1 and Τi2, it is possible to have a chain of treatment effects, as in Τi1, Τ1j, Τ2j, ..., Τnj, Τi2 such that two adjoining treatments in the chain occur in the same block

Experimental design

We can define λ in terms of our design parameters when we have equal block size k, and equal replication ri = r. For a given set of t, k, and r we define λ as:

 

λ = r(k-1) / t-1

 

So, for the first example that we looked at earlier - let's plug in the values and calculate λ:

 

λ = 3 (3 - 1) / (4 -1) = 2

 

Here is the key: when λ is equal to an integer number it tells us that a balanced incomplete block design exists. Let's look at the second example and use the formula and plug in the values for this second example. So, for t = 4, k = 2, r = 2 and b = 4, we have:

 

λ = 2 (2 - 1) / (4 - 1) = 0.666

 

Since λ is not an integer there does not exist a balanced incomplete block design for this experiment. We would either need more replicates or a larger block size. Seeing as how the block size in this case is fixed, we can achieve a balanced complete block design by adding more replicates so that λ equals at least 1. It needs to be a whole number in order for the design to be balanced.

This will be the first of a two-part series that explores the the role of theory in research. First I will provide a brief introduction about theory and why we might want to think about it, then I will discuss the decisions that face advanced prostate cancer patients, and propose a middle-range theory to help us understand this phenomenon and guide research. In the second part I will step through an application of the theory, and then provide analysis and evaluation of the theory using Fawcett's criteria.

Introduction

Why theory?

Hempel (1966) provides the following definition (p. 15)

[Scientific theories and hypotheses] constitute guesses at the connections that might obtain between the phenomena under study, at uniformities and patterns that might underlie their occurrence
Hempel provides this definition that suggests theories serve to explain causal mechanisms in the world around us, they are a way to look at events in the world, explain them, and use those explanations to predict future events. It is important to note that not everyone agrees with Hempel's definiton, but it will be useful guide for this presentation. As Hempel suggests, one of the major ways is by providing researchers to formalize their empirical observations in the form of a model of the world, then see how well that model generates prospective predictions. Theories can also be hypothesis generating, meaning that postulating a model of the world can implicitly suggest relationships that can then be tested empirically. Theory may also be a good way of stopping ourselves from providing post-hoc rationalizations for how events are related by making us specify what we think, then test it.

This diagram is adapted from the website listed at the bottom, which is a blog post written in 2011 by an archeologist. At the bottom is concrete reality and as we move upward each level becomes more abstract. As our representations become more abstract we are able to provide a greater breadth of explanation, but there is naturally less empircal content. When we conduct research, it is important to be able to connect each of these levels. We would like to think that our data represent some slice of reality, and we need a conceptual model for how the data we collect relate to the real world, a middle-range theory is a way of postulating a set of relationships between a limited set of variables or phenomena, grand theories tend to be more of a theory-of-everything, like the way cognitive science tries to explain the mind. From the Stanford Encyclopedia: "The central hypothesis of cognitive science is that thinking can best be understood in terms of representational structures in the mind and computational procedures that operate on those structures." Middle range theories can be especially helpful in nursing research due to their scope. A middle range theory can provide a relatively comprehensive account of domain specific phenomena, while going beyond the level of detail of a conceptual model. Notice that reality is enclosed in scare quotes. That is because the nature of reality is hotly disputed in the academic literature. This is beyond the scope of this presentation, but I would add one more level to this diagram, [go forward] epistemology is the study of the nature of knowledge, as in, how do we know what we know and what does that mean, and ontology is the study of the nature of reality, like, if a tree falls in the forest and no one is around to hear it, does it make a sound? These fields of study are related to the philosophy of science, and they are also important to think about. Link: https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2008/entries/cognitive-science/
